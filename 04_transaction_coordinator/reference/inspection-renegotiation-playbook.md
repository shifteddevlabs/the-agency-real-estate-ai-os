# Inspection Renegotiation Playbook

How to handle the gap between "inspection report received" and "buyer's final decision" during the option period.

## What This Covers

After a Texas residential inspection, the buyer has four paths:

1. Accept the property as-is and proceed.
2. Request repairs via amendment.
3. Request a credit at closing (price reduction or seller contribution).
4. Terminate during the option period.

This playbook helps the transaction coordinator support Diana in framing the conversation, drafting amendments, and tracking what's been requested.

## Step One: Triage The Report

When an inspection report comes in, sort findings into three buckets:

| Bucket | Examples | Typical Response |
|---|---|---|
| Material / safety | Foundation movement, active leak, electrical hazard, gas leak, structural failure, major roof damage | Request repair OR credit; possibly terminate |
| Functional / cost | HVAC near end of life, water heater old, minor plumbing, GFCI issues, drainage concerns | Request repair, credit, or accept depending on price |
| Cosmetic / informational | Caulking, screens, paint touch-ups, normal wear, recommendations not defects | Usually accept; document for future reference |

Do NOT classify items yourself. Surface the inspector's findings and let Diana and the client decide. The role here is structure, not judgment.

## Step Two: Decide The Path Based On Severity

The decision isn't yours to make. But Diana will ask "how do we frame this for the client?" — provide the structure, not the recommendation.

Framing options to surface:

- **Path A: Accept as-is.** Move forward, no amendment. Often chosen for cosmetic-only reports or in competitive markets where renegotiation risks the deal.
- **Path B: Request repairs.** Seller fixes specific items before closing. Requires inspection-of-repairs visit before close.
- **Path C: Request a credit.** Seller reduces price OR contributes to closing costs. Buyer handles repairs after closing. Often preferred because: (a) buyer controls the work, (b) no quality-of-repair disputes, (c) credits at closing reduce buyer's cash-to-close. Lender may cap how much can be applied to closing costs vs. price.
- **Path D: Terminate.** Within the option period, no penalty (lose option fee only). After option ends, very difficult and usually requires legal escalation.

## Step Three: Draft The Amendment

If the buyer requests repairs or a credit, the buyer's agent drafts a TREC 39 Amendment (current version) listing:

- Specific items to be repaired (use inspector's exact language where possible).
- Or specific dollar amount for credit.
- Who pays for re-inspection if applicable.
- Deadline for completion.

Surface the draft for Diana's review. Never send to the seller's side without Diana's approval.

## Step Four: Track The Response

Sellers have three responses:

1. Agree as proposed. Sign and return.
2. Counter. Reduce scope, change dollar amount, or modify terms.
3. Reject. No repairs, no credit.

Each response is a fresh negotiation step. The clock keeps running on the option period. If the seller drags their feet, the buyer's option may end before resolution. In that case the buyer must decide before 5 PM on the last option day:

- Terminate (recover earnest money, lose option fee).
- Accept as-is (proceed knowing the seller said no).
- Submit a fresh termination notice and try to renegotiate from outside the contract (very risky, usually doesn't work).

## Step Five: Verify Repairs Before Closing

If repairs were agreed to:

- Schedule the inspection of repairs 5 to 7 days before closing.
- Get receipts and warranties from the seller for all completed work.
- If repairs are incomplete or substandard, raise it with Diana immediately. Closing may need to be delayed or the credit converted at closing.

## What To Never Do

- Tell the client what the inspector "really meant."
- Estimate repair costs without contractor quotes.
- Suggest the property is "good" or "bad" overall.
- Agree to a contract amendment without Diana's review.
- Promise the seller will fix something before they've agreed.
- Recommend waiving inspection contingencies.
- Recommend waiving the inspection-of-repairs visit.

## Output Format

When supporting Diana during inspection renegotiation, produce:

```md
## Inspection Summary
- Report received:
- Major findings (buyer's words or inspector's words, not yours):
- Specialist evaluation needed: yes / no / which trade

## Path Options
- Accept as-is: implications
- Request repairs: which items, why
- Request credit: amount range, why
- Terminate: deadline, what's lost

## Recommended Surface (not recommendation)
[a clean summary for Diana to discuss with the buyer]

## Draft Amendment (if proceeding)
[TREC 39 language, ready for Diana's review]

## Tracking
- Amendment delivered:
- Seller response deadline (option end):
- Re-inspection scheduled:
- Diana approved:

## Client Update Draft Points
[bullets for handoff to 03_client_communication]
```

## Compliance Notes

- The inspector is the source of truth on findings. Do not paraphrase severity.
- Repair cost estimates come from licensed contractors, not the system.
- Repair scope disputes can become legal disputes. Escalate to broker or attorney early.
- Do not advise the buyer to use the inspection report as leverage on items outside the report.
